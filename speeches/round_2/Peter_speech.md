# Peter's Speech – Round 2

## **From Diagnosis to Architecture: Building a Scalable, Adaptive Policy Bridge**

Colleagues, I thank both Mary and Paul for their profound contributions. Mary rightly elevates the human dimension—the crisis of meaning, identity, and equity that pure economic metrics miss. Paul correctly sounds the alarm on velocity—the “startup speed” of AI deployment that threatens to overwhelm our 20th-century institutions. Both perspectives are essential, and I agree: we face a challenge that is simultaneously economic, social, and technological.

However, as an economist and policy maker, my role is to translate these urgent truths into a **feasible, fundable, and flexible policy architecture**. The research from Rounds 1 and 2 provides us not just with warnings, but with a blueprint. The central insight is this: **The problem is not a net lack of wealth or jobs, but a catastrophic mismatch in their distribution across time, geography, and skill sets.** Our task is to build a bridge that allows society to cross this chasm without leaving millions behind.

---

## **The Three Pillars of a "Just Transition" Bridge**

Drawing from the most effective models in our research—Scandinavian flexicurity, German sectoral partnerships, and Singaporean human capital strategy—I propose a tripartite policy framework: **Cushion, Catalyze, and Connect.**

### **1. Cushion: Immediate and Adaptive Safety Nets**

Mary speaks of dignity. The first blow to dignity is economic freefall. We must modernize our safety nets to match the nature of AI displacement.

*   **Wage Insurance, Not Just Unemployment:** As our Round 2 research shows, the U.S. Trade Adjustment Assistance model is inconsistent, but the principle is sound. We should implement a **National Transition Benefit**. For workers displaced by automation who take a lower-paying job, this benefit would cover, for example, 70% of their wage loss for up to two years. The MIT CSAIL finding that most jobs are *partially* automatable is key here—this benefit supports workers through transitions to new roles or reduced hours, not just total job loss.
*   **Portable Benefits for a Hybrid Workforce:** We must decouple health care, pensions, and training credits from any single employer. This provides security for gig workers, part-time learners, and those piecing together income from multiple AI-augmented roles.
*   **Psychological Support as Standard Policy:** Mary’s emphasis on the identity crisis is backed by data. The Harvard study showing a 3x higher depression rate for those whose jobs are fully automated is a policy input, not just a social observation. Retraining programs must be bundled with counseling and community support, as seen in the successful Swedish truck driver case study.

This "Cushion" pillar is non-negotiable. It is the social shock absorber that allows for risk-taking and learning.

### **2. Catalyze: Strategic Investment in Human-AI Symbiosis**

Paul fears we are building AI to replace, not augment. Policy can change those incentives and accelerate the creation of new, high-quality work.

*   **Tax Policy for Augmentation, Not Just Automation:** We should reform corporate taxation to encourage human-AI collaboration. Instead of a blunt "robot tax," consider an **"Augmentation Incentive Credit."** Companies that invest in systems that augment worker productivity (with verifiable metrics for job quality and wage growth) receive significant tax benefits. Conversely, we can phase out tax deductions for equipment that purely displaces workers without a transition plan, as proposed in bipartisan U.S. discussions.
*   **Fund the "Human" Sectors:** The market alone will not adequately value care, education, and community arts—sectors where human presence is irreplaceable and social value immense. Public investment here, akin to a **Job Guarantee in the Caring Economy**, creates a buffer of meaningful employment. The ILO data shows women are disproportionately at risk; investing in the care economy directly counters this inequity.
*   **Sectoral Transition Councils:** The most effective retraining programs, with a 78% employment rate, are sectoral partnerships. We need state-facilitated councils in high-risk industries (e.g., transportation, administrative support) where unions, employers, and educators jointly design curricula for the future of their own industry. Germany’s model proves this works.

### **3. Connect: Lifelong Infrastructure for Learning and Mobility**

The bridge must have on-ramps for everyone, everywhere. This is the most complex engineering challenge.

*   **Individual Learning Accounts (ILAs):** Singapore’s SkillsFuture is the gold standard. Every citizen should have a publicly-funded, lifelong learning account. Our research shows short, generic workshops fail. ILAs fund meaningful, stackable credentials co-designed by industry.
*   **The Digital Infrastructure Imperative:** It is a cruel paradox to offer online retraining to the 30% of rural America without adequate broadband. **Universal broadband is as essential as roads** for the 21st-century labor market. This is a prerequisite for any geographically fair transition.
*   **Mobility Stipends & Community Anchors:** For workers in regions facing systemic automation—the 15 U.S. counties with >40% high-risk jobs identified by Brookings—we need support for geographic mobility. For those who stay, we must invest in "community anchor" institutions like colleges and libraries to become local hubs for remote work and retraining.

## **Addressing the Inevitable Objections: Feasibility and Funding**

The scale is daunting. The EU estimates a need for 2-3% of GDP annually. The political will seems scarce. Here is the economic case:

1.  **The Cost of Inaction is Far Higher:** The German *Kurzarbeit* model costs €25,000 per worker retained, compared to €45,000 in unemployment benefits and lost taxes. **Preventative policy is cheaper than remedial crisis management.** The social costs of despair, as Mary outlined, create downstream burdens on health and justice systems.
2.  **Fund from the AI Dividend Itself:** The PwC projection is crucial: AI could boost global GDP by 14-26%. A small reallocation of this massive new wealth can fund the transition. Mechanisms could include:
    *   A modest levy on the commercialization of large-scale, labor-displacing AI systems.
    *   Re-directing a portion of corporate productivity gains from AI toward a sovereign transition fund, modeled on the Alaska Permanent Fund.
    *   Reforming capital gains taxes to better capture returns to automation.

This is not anti-innovation; it is **pro-social stability**. It ensures the market has consumers with income and societies with cohesion.

---

## **Conclusion: A Call for Pragmatic Optimism**

Mary, Paul—you have both framed the stakes perfectly. We are navigating a **velocity crisis** (Paul’s point) that threatens to cause a **meaning crisis** (Mary’s point).

My synthesis as a policy maker is this: We must build a bridge with three lanes:
*   A **protected lane** (Cushion) for those most vulnerable to the immediate shocks.
*   An **express lane** (Catalyze) for investing in the human-AI partnerships of tomorrow.
*   And a **on-ramp lane** (Connect) that ensures every person and every community has access to the journey.

The research shows us what works: sectoral partnerships, wage insurance, lifelong learning accounts. The challenge is not knowledge, but will. We must choose to architect this bridge with the same ingenuity and investment we apply to the AI systems themselves. The goal is not just to avoid mass unemployment, but to steward a transition to a future where AI elevates human potential rather than replaces it.

Let us build that bridge. Thank you.
