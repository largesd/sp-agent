# Mary’s Speech – Round 1  
**Role: Sociologist / Labor Rights Advocate**

## **Beyond Productivity: Reclaiming Work as a Social Good in the Age of AI**

Colleagues, Peter and Paul have rightly emphasized the scale and speed of AI-driven disruption. Peter speaks of policy architecture; Paul warns of “startup speed” outpacing our institutions. Both are correct—but incomplete. As a sociologist and labor rights advocate, I argue that **we cannot solve mass unemployment without first redefining what work *means* in a post-AI society**.  

Technology does not operate in a vacuum. It reflects our values, our power structures, and our moral choices. And right now, AI is being deployed not to liberate human potential, but to extract labor value faster and more cheaply. The result? Not just job loss—but the **erosion of dignity, community, and purpose** that work has historically provided.

---

## **The Human Cost of “Efficiency-First” AI**

Let’s be clear: AI is not like the steam engine or the ATM. As Paul noted, it automates *judgment*, *creativity*, and *empathy*—the very qualities that make us human. A 2023 Goldman Sachs report estimates that **300 million full-time jobs globally could be exposed to automation**, with white-collar professionals—paralegals, radiologists, customer service reps—not spared.  

But the deeper crisis lies in *who* bears the brunt. Kate Crawford’s research shows that **women, racial minorities, and low-income workers are disproportionately represented in roles most vulnerable to AI displacement**. Why? Because these are often the “invisible” cognitive and emotional labor roles—scheduling, moderating, caring—that AI systems are now trained to replicate using data extracted from underpaid human workers.  

This isn’t neutral progress. It’s **algorithmic stratification**—a new form of inequality encoded in code.

---

## **Historical Lessons: Transition Requires More Than Tech or Training**

Peter points to lifelong learning as a solution. But history cautions us. During the Industrial Revolution, it took **generations**—and mass social movements, unions, and public education—to absorb displaced labor. The ATM case study is often misused: yes, bank teller jobs didn’t vanish, but only because banks *chose* to redeploy workers into sales roles. That was a **business decision**, not an automatic market outcome.

Today, AI’s disruption is **faster, broader, and less geographically contained**. A factory closing in Ohio could be mirrored by a call center shutdown in Manila and a legal research firm in Bangalore—all within months. Retraining programs like the U.S. Trade Adjustment Assistance have **placement rates below 50%**, especially for mid-career workers. Telling someone to “learn to code” at 50, after decades in logistics or administration, ignores the **social, psychological, and structural barriers** to such transitions.

## **A Sociological Framework for the Post-AI Labor Market**

We need more than skills—we need **social infrastructure**. I propose a three-part vision grounded in labor rights and human dignity:

### **1. Guarantee Work That Machines Cannot Do—Because It’s Human**

David Autor reminds us that jobs are bundles of tasks. While AI may handle data analysis, it cannot provide the **empathy of a nurse**, the **mentorship of a teacher**, or the **solidarity of a community organizer**. These are not “fallback” jobs—they are **core social goods**.

We must therefore **strategically invest in high-human-touch sectors**: elder care, mental health, early childhood education, climate resilience. Daron Acemoglu calls this “**directional technological change**”—steering innovation toward tasks that *create* human roles, not eliminate them. A federal **job guarantee** in these sectors would not only absorb displaced workers but elevate undervalued care work, long dominated by women and people of color.

### **2. Redistribute the Gains—Because Productivity ≠ Prosperity**

Goldman Sachs predicts AI could boost global GDP by 7%. But as Erik Brynjolfsson warns, we’re in a **“productivity paradox”**: gains flow to capital, not labor. Since 1980, U.S. productivity has risen 62%, but median wages only 17%. AI will widen this gap unless we act.

We must **tax the automation that displaces workers**—as Bill Gates proposed—and redirect those funds into **Social Wealth Funds** that distribute AI dividends universally. South Korea already reduces tax incentives for firms that over-automate. We can go further: mandate **AI impact assessments**, requiring companies to disclose job displacement risks before deployment, just as we do for environmental harm.

### **3. Redefine Security Beyond Wages—Because Survival Shouldn’t Depend on a Boss**

Paul speaks of “startup speed,” but human lives aren’t MVPs. We must decouple basic survival from wage labor. Universal Basic Income (UBI) pilots—from Finland to Stockton—show **no significant drop in employment**, but more importantly, they reduce anxiety, improve health, and empower workers to say “no” to exploitative gigs.

Yet UBI alone is insufficient. As a sociologist, I know that **work provides identity, rhythm, and belonging**. That’s why we must pair income support with **Universal Basic Services**: guaranteed healthcare, housing, transit, and broadband. When people aren’t desperate, they can retrain meaningfully, organize collectively, and demand better jobs—not just any job.

---

## **A Call for Democratic Control Over Technology**

Finally, we must democratize AI’s design. In Germany, **worker councils co-determine** how automation is implemented. Why not require **labor representation on AI development teams**? Why not fund **public AI labs** focused on augmentation, not replacement?

This isn’t anti-technology. It’s **pro-humanity**. We have a choice: let AI deepen inequality and alienation, or harness it to build a society where everyone has the **time, security, and dignity** to contribute meaningfully.

---

## **Conclusion: The Choice Is Ours**

The post-AI era doesn’t have to mean mass unemployment. But avoiding it requires more than clever algorithms or vocational courses. It demands **moral courage**—to value care over consumption, people over profits, and community over convenience.

Let us not wait for markets to “correct.” Let us not romanticize disruption. Let us build a future where technology serves human flourishing—not the other way around.

**The question isn’t whether AI will change work. It’s whether we will change society to ensure that work—paid or unpaid, human or assisted—remains a source of dignity, not despair.**  

Thank you.
